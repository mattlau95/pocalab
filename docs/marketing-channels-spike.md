# MAT-375 — Marketing Channels Spike

Pocalab lets anyone build a deck of custom photocards — upload photos, crop to 55×85mm, set quantities, download a print-ready PDF. The product is physical-media-first and fan-culture-native, which makes it well suited for communities that already trade, gift, and collect small-format cards. This spike evaluates the most promising outreach channels: who they are, why the fit exists, and how to approach them without a large marketing budget.

---

## Channels

### 1. K-pop Shops

**Who they are:** Small independent retailers (online or brick-and-mortar) that sell K-pop merchandise — albums, lightsticks, photocards, and fan-made goods.

**Why they'd buy:** Many K-pop shops include free "freebies" with orders (photocards, stickers, postcards) as a customer loyalty tactic. Custom photocards are exactly what they already source — pocalab could be a faster, cheaper, lower-MOQ alternative to their current print vendor.

**Outreach approach:** Find shops on Instagram or TikTok Shop and DM directly. Lead with a free sample print. Offer a shop-specific discount code they can use for reorders.

**Effort:** Low — DM-based, no gatekeeping  
**Opportunity:** High — recurring print orders, natural fit with existing freebie culture

---

### 2. K-pop Fan Events & Coffee Events

**Who they are:** Fan-organized events celebrating a specific artist — anniversary parties, birthday cafes, and "coffee events" where fans decorate a café with artist photos and give freebies to visitors.

**Why they'd buy:** Coffee events are freebie-heavy by design. Organizers print dozens to hundreds of photocards to hand out. They often make these themselves at home with poor quality. Pocalab offers a print-ready PDF they can take to a local printer or order as a batch.

**Outreach approach:** Monitor Twitter/X and Instagram hashtags like `#coffeevent`, `#birthdaycafe`, `[artist]` + "fansite." Reach out to organizers 4–6 weeks before event dates when they're in production mode. Offer to sponsor a small batch in exchange for a shoutout.

**Effort:** Medium — requires timing and monitoring  
**Opportunity:** High — concentrated demand, word-of-mouth among organizers is fast

---

### 3. K-pop Traders

**Who they are:** Fans who trade physical photocards with each other, primarily on Twitter/X, Reddit, and TikTok. Some traders have large followings and run high-volume swap threads.

**Why they'd buy:** Traders need filler cards, PC-for-PC trades, and custom "wanted" cards. Being able to print a custom photocard of a rare or unreleased image is genuinely novel and desirable in that community.

**Outreach approach:** Post in trading subreddits (r/KpopPhotoCards) and Twitter trading communities with a demo showing a before/after — raw photo to finished printed card. Pin the post with a link to pocalab. Partner with a mid-tier trading account for a sponsored post.

**Effort:** Low — community posting, no budget required  
**Opportunity:** Medium — individual order sizes are small, but community reach is large

---

### 4. Anime Shops

**Who they are:** Shops selling anime merchandise — figures, dakimakura, art prints, and fan goods. Many overlap with J-pop, manga, and cosplay communities.

**Why they'd buy:** Anime shops sell printed goods (art cards, bromides, character cards) and many carry fan-made doujin items. Custom character cards are a natural extension. Some shops would reorder in bulk for their own inventory.

**Outreach approach:** Reach out to shops at anime conventions (Artist Alley) first — lower barrier than approaching established stores cold. Offer a convention-exclusive discount to gauge demand before pursuing wholesale conversations.

**Effort:** Medium — convention scheduling required  
**Opportunity:** Medium — broader product scope (not card-native), but convention presence generates samples and social proof

---

### 5. Party Event Planners

**Who they are:** Professional or semi-professional planners who organize themed birthday parties, quinceañeras, bachelorette parties, and similar events.

**Why they'd buy:** Custom photocards work as party favors — personalized cards with the guest of honor's photo. This is a non-fan use case that still maps exactly to the product. Planners who find a reliable tool recommend it repeatedly to clients.

**Outreach approach:** Post in Facebook groups for event planners and party supply communities. Create a short demo video showing a "party favor" card made with pocalab and post to Pinterest and TikTok (both are heavily used by party planners for ideas).

**Effort:** Medium — requires content creation for Pinterest/TikTok  
**Opportunity:** Medium — less culturally native to photocards, but total addressable market is much larger

---

### 6. Wedding Coordinators

**Who they are:** Wedding planners and coordinators who source custom printed goods — place cards, escort cards, favor boxes, and keepsake items.

**Why they'd buy:** Photocard-sized prints (55×85mm) are close to standard business card or escort card size. A custom photocard of the couple makes a distinctive, print-at-home-friendly favor or escort card alternative. The no-account, instant-PDF workflow is a strong sell for time-crunched planners.

**Outreach approach:** Post on wedding planning forums (The Knot, WeddingWire community) and wedding subreddits (r/weddingplanning). Target "DIY wedding favors" keyword on Pinterest with a board of photocard favor examples.

**Effort:** Medium — SEO/Pinterest content strategy  
**Opportunity:** Low–Medium — requires repositioning from "kpop" to "photo keepsake"; harder to reach at scale, but average order value is higher
